Prepare for your Meeting or Visit

Every community or group has unique protocols, customs and traditions as part of its specific cultural heritage. Moreover, there are cultural differences between and within First Nations, Inuit and Métis communities. Before you visit a community or host a meeting with Indigenous guests, be sure to first contact the community to find out what is required to ensure respect for proper protocol.

It is important to communicate what you hope to achieve in advance of the meeting. It is easier to arrange a meeting if officials know what you want to discuss and why.

Engage with the community representatives as early as possible from the inception of the idea, initiative or project. By initiating contact from the outset, you foster the active participation of all parties and demonstrate a genuine willingness to work collaboratively.

To engage with an Indigenous community, send a meeting request in writing. Contact the chief and council directly in the case of an official visit; depending on the purpose of the visit and the community, other officials may also need to be contacted. Follow up with a phone call to the band administrator or appropriate representative.

Some remote Indigenous communities may not have access to reliable broadband internet service, so it is important to always follow up with a telephone call after communicating with them by email.

You should contact each community individually. Bear in mind that communities often have capacity issues and setting up a meeting can take some time.

There are a few things to consider once initial contact has been established. We will explore these aspects in later sections of the present document.

Do Your Homework +

Confirm Your Visit

Following the initial acceptance of your invitation by the First Nation, Inuit or Métis community or group, be sure to follow up to confirm the meeting one week in advance of the visit and then again the day before.

Be Flexible

Officials have hectic schedules, so it’s not uncommon for a meeting to be interrupted, delayed or canceled.

While you may request to meet with the chief and council or other leader of the community, your meeting may in fact take place with the portfolio holder (usually an elected councilor) or a non-elected band administrator such as the executive director to the chief. This is analogous to an Indigenous group meeting with a director rather than the minister.

Check on the timing of site visits or presentations, as well as the availability of community members, well in advance. Also, note that:

  • Community offices will often close down for a day or two for important community events
  • Some communities have important cultural and traditional events such as seasonal hunting or harvesting that will take a priority over your meeting
  • In the event of a death in the community, meetings may be postponed until the community grieving process has passed
  • Scheduling weekend or evening events may also be challenging due to community pastimes or activities

Also, ensure flexibility due to unforeseen weather conditions and dress accordingly.

Get to Know the Current Reality

Understanding the current reality of the community or group you are about to visit is very important. Before you visit or host members of a community or group:

  • Conduct some fact-finding research on the First Nation, Inuit or Métis community or group you are about to work with or visit to learn about significant or relevant current or past events
  • Learn about their history, governance structure and cultural and traditional practices
    • Is the community’s territory covered by a treaty or historic agreement? Historic treaties, including early peace and friendship treaties, numbered treaties and modern-day comprehensive land claims agreements or Aboriginal self-government agreements may apply
    • Is there a person or group responsible for your portfolio/file within the community? If so, who are they?
  • Be aware of the nature of the current relationship between the community and the government
  • Verify if other government or private-sector representatives have recently collaborated with the community’s leaders. If so, inquire whether or not they covered topics related to those you wish to discuss and what the outcomes were. Some communities may be already engaged in negotiations at the provincial, territorial or federal levels on any number of matters, and this could influence whether or not they want to engage with other federal organizations on other matters due to strategic timing or other considerations
  • Learn how to pronounce the names of the community and the people you will meet

Confirm the Elder’s Role

Ask if a community Elder will be or should be opening and/or closing the meeting.

Learn about the Elder and their role in the community, as the definition of an Elder varies between First Nations, Inuit and Métis. You will feel more at ease if you familiarize yourself with the different practices of the community you are visiting.

Visit the Kumik Lodge or the Iskotew Lodge to get additional information and guidance on Elders and their role.

Ask what opening and closing ceremonies will be performed; there may be a prayer, traditional song and drum, a smudging, lighting of the qulliq or pipe ceremony, among other practices or traditions. Following the traditional spiritual protocols of the community is important in many Indigenous communities. Confirm with the community representatives.

Note: Should you be asked to take part in a smudging ceremony, a pipe ceremony, a sweat lodge or a traditional dance, be mindful that this invitation was not extended to you lightly. Such an invitation is an honour, and unless there is a medical or other substantive reason why you should not join in, it is strongly encouraged that you accept with humility and respect.

It is important to ask about the protocols for participating in ceremonies. For example, there are certain ceremonies that are specific to women and to men. If you are not sure, do not be afraid to ask the Elder for guidance on the purpose of the ceremony and what is expected of you.

The Elder's Role +

Present an Offering to an Elder

It is highly recommended that you present an offering to the Elder to thank them for their time and to recognize the value of their knowledge. This is not to be considered a form of payment, but a gesture of respect.

Find out who to talk to within the host community regarding an appropriate offering. Tobacco is widely accepted amongst many First Nations and Métis. In many Inuit communities, an offering of tea (herbal tea, blueberry tea) may be appreciated.

Traditional plant or herbal offerings vary from region to region across Canada. These may include shredded cedar bark on the Northwest coast, sage on the Prairies, tobacco around the Great Lakes, and sweetgrass elsewhere. Offerings of whole or natural foods may also be appropriate, especially in Inuit communities. Depending on the region of the country, these may also include canned or smoked salmon or other fish; wild rice; or blueberries, strawberries or cranberries in various preparations (e.g. jams or dried).

Consult with your procurement or financial advisors prior to deciding on or purchasing an offering, as practices may vary from one department to another.

Honorarium

A professional service fee, financial honorarium or other remuneration is often also provided to the Elder performing the opening and closing ceremony for an event or providing additional services such as moderating or counselling throughout the day.

Determine the Elder’s fee before the meeting. Consider travel costs, meal allowances, parking and the number of hours they will provide.

Verify if your department has developed guidelines related to Indigenous Elders’ professional service fees or financial honorariums.

Did You Know

There are more than 618 First Nations communities in Canada, representing more than 50 nations and 50 Indigenous languages.

Three-quarters of Inuit in Canada live in 53 communities across the northern regions of the country in Inuit Nunangat, which means "the place where Inuit live." Inuit Nunangat is made up of 4 regions: Inuvialuit (Northwest Territories and Yukon), Nunavut, Nunavik (Northern Quebec) and Nunatsiavut (Labrador).

Métis are one of three recognized Aboriginal peoples in Canada. According to the 2011 National Household Survey, 418,380 Canadians self-identified as Métis.

SOURCE: Indigenous and Northern Affairs Canada, 2017.
