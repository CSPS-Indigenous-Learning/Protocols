Introduction

A solid understanding and acknowledgment of cultural protocols is vital to establishing meaningful and lasting working relationships with Indigenous communities and groups—relationships based on mutual respect and trust as a matter of practice. Knowledge of the importance of the protocols that are unique and specific to a community, group or culture will help build trust and lead to beneficial outcomes for all parties involved.

This guide is intended to prepare you to interact with Indigenous communities and groups and ensure that you appropriately follow up with your contacts to maintain positive working relationships going forward.

As a public servant, you may be asked to work with Indigenous communities, groups or associations for a wide variety of reasons, including, but not limited to the following:

  • fulfilling the Duty to Consult
  • soliciting advice and guidance
  • learning from Elders and other members of the community
  • discussing a project
  • supporting the delivery of services

Whatever the reason, establish contact early on, and remember: acknowledging protocols is the first step to developing any respectful intercultural relationship.

What Is "Cultural Protocol"?

Cultural protocols are the etiquette, customs, codes, and other behaviours of a particular cultural community or group and the appropriate processes for conducting business with that group.

Although protocols share some common themes and practices, it is important to recognize the great diversity that exists among Indigenous peoples and communities, who each have their own culture, heritage and language—all of which influence proper protocol.

Indigenous traditions and ethical conduct are important to maintaining networks of kinship, establishing respectful relationships, and exercising environmental and public service stewardship.
